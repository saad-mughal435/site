Engineering case study

Building a production MES/ERP at Kingsley Beverage

A single operations platform that replaced spreadsheets and paper across five departments of a Dubai beverage plant - designed, built and run by one developer, in production since 2025. Published with Kingsley's permission; every specific below is sanitized, so there are no real customers, suppliers, brands or production figures here.

01The problem: a plant run from spreadsheets

Before the platform, the plant ran on Excel files and paper. Production plans lived in one workbook; QC seam-check and batch records on clipboards; inventory and expiry in another spreadsheet; dispatch on printed sheets; and the daily numbers were re-keyed by hand into a management report each morning. None of it was wrong, exactly - it was just spread across files that did not talk to each other, owned by different people, and reconciled by memory. The same quantity got typed three times into three documents, and when they disagreed, someone spent the start of their day finding out which was right.

The cost was not one big failure but a steady tax: time lost re-entering data, batch and expiry traceability that depended on a single person remembering where the sheet was, and a morning report that was only as fresh as whoever had finished typing. The goal was narrow and concrete - one system of record for the plant's operations, with the daily report falling out of it automatically rather than being assembled by hand.

02Constraints that shaped every decision

  • The machine layer is OEM-locked

    The production lines are Krones - superb, and entirely closed. The PLC layer is the vendor's domain, warranty-locked, and not something a plant developer should touch. So the platform lives around the automation, not inside it: it captures production, downtime, QC and batch data and turns it into reports, while the machines keep running the line.

    Trade-off: no direct telemetry from the PLCs; operator-entered data is the source of truth, so the forms have to be trustworthy.

  • One developer, end to end

    Data model, backend, frontend, deployment, ops and support are all mine. That rules out anything that needs a team to keep alive - exotic infrastructure, a microservice sprawl, a build pipeline only one consultant understands. The bias is toward boring, durable choices I can debug at 6 a.m. without a second opinion.

    Trade-off: deliberately conservative tech; cleverness is a liability when there is no one to hand it to.

  • The users are operators, mid-shift

    This software is read by a night-shift operator handing over at 6 a.m., on a shared terminal next to a machine, while three things need attention. One screen per question, big numbers first, state that survives an interruption.

    Trade-off: fewer fields, fewer features - every extra input is a tax collected on every unit, every shift, forever.

  • It has to beat paper, honestly

    Paper boots instantly, never loses a session and survives a network outage. A digital form does not win by being digital; it wins by being at least as fast to fill in and then doing what paper cannot - totalling itself, flagging the out-of-range value, landing in the report without being retyped.

    Trade-off: if a form is slower than the clipboard, the floor quietly goes back to the clipboard - so speed beat polish.

03Architecture

The shape is deliberately simple: role-specific web UIs for each department feed a set of FastAPI services, which read and write two stores - MongoDB for the document-shaped operational data (production logs, QC records, batches) and SQL Server where relational integrity and the accounts side need it. A custom adapter syncs the relevant records into Sage Evolution so finance keeps its system of record. Reports are rendered server-side as PDFs - six templates - because the report is what management actually opens. The whole thing runs in Docker on a Linux VM, behind a Cloudflare-fronted nginx.

04Key decisions & trade-offs

  • The report is the product, so PDFs are first-class

    The GM does not log into a dashboard at 7 a.m.; they open the PDF in their inbox. So the database, the APIs and the admin screens all exist to make one correct, consistent, on-time document arrive. Effort went into report layouts that scan in thirty seconds and numbers that reconcile across departments.

    Trade-off: heavier investment in a server-side PDF pipeline than a typical CRUD app, for a deliverable some would treat as an afterthought.

  • Compute the numbers at close, not on a cron

    Figures are computed at the event - when a job or shift closes, from final inputs - not by a scheduled sweep of the tables. A report pulled at 7:00 and one pulled at 7:20 must agree, or the floor stops trusting the number. Computing once, at close, makes every figure stable and auditable.

    Trade-off: the close transition carries more logic and has to be correct under interruption, instead of a simpler "recalculate everything periodically".

  • The UI mirrors the five departments

    Roles are a domain concept, not a security afterthought: an operator logs production, a supervisor approves it, finance reads it. The interface is partitioned the way the plant is, so each user sees their job and nothing else - which is also what keeps the data clean.

    Trade-off: more screens to build and maintain than a single shared view, traded for far lower training cost and fewer wrong entries.

  • FIFO inventory as an enforced invariant

    Raw material is consumed against jobs in arrival order, with partial lots - exactly where spreadsheet logic quietly breaks, and exactly the kind of rule the data layer should own rather than trust a human to follow. Expiry tracking rides on the same lot model.

    Trade-off: a stricter inventory model that sometimes refuses a shortcut an operator wants - the right friction for traceability.

  • Sage stays the system of record for finance

    Rather than rebuild accounting, a custom adapter syncs the relevant records into Sage Evolution, so finance keeps the tool they audit in and the plant data lands there without re-keying.

    Trade-off: a integration surface to own and keep in sync, accepted to avoid reinventing - and re-certifying - an accounting system.

Estimate, not a measurement The platform cut production reporting time by an estimated ~60%. I say "estimated" deliberately: the before-state was spread across spreadsheets, paper and people's evenings, and nobody ever timed it with a stopwatch. There is no clean baseline to subtract from, so the figure is a considered estimate of the morning re-keying and reconciliation that the system removed - not a benchmark. The factory floor has a finely tuned detector for inflated numbers, and the fastest way to lose the room is to claim precision you do not have.

What is not an estimate: the system is in production, all five departments run on it daily, and the morning report is generated rather than assembled. The honest headline is that the plant now has one place where its operational truth lives.

06What I would do differently, and next

With hindsight I would split the two data stores along a cleaner line from the start - Mongo and SQL Server each ended up holding things that, in retrospect, belonged on the other side, and the boundary is something I have since had to tend. I would also write the downtime reason codes as a first-class taxonomy on day one rather than letting them grow: a single "downtime minutes" number answers "how bad", but only categorised reasons answer "what do we fix first".

Next on the list is automated test coverage around the close-of-shift computations and the Sage sync - the two places where a silent error is most expensive - and tighter monitoring so "the report did not arrive" is caught by a check rather than by the GM. Some of that thinking already lives in the open: I rebuilt this domain publicly as ShopFloor API, a Spring Boot OEE backend with the compute-at-close state machine, FIFO inventory and Testcontainers tests - the same problems, solved where anyone can read the solution.
